Reading less, and knowing when to stop.

Continued-stay authorization in behavioral health normally means reviewing every document in a patient's chart. This system reads them one at a time, ranks what to read next, accumulates evidence as a likelihood ratio, and halts the moment the evidence crosses a decision boundary. In practice it stops after four of eleven document types.

What the system does

An LLM ranks which document type is likely to be most informative next, given what has already been read. A Python likelihood accumulator — not the model — holds the evidence state and applies the stopping rule. Separating the two matters: the ranking is a heuristic that can be wrong without compromising the decision, because the accumulation and the boundary are deterministic and auditable.

The design target is documentation burden. Clinical staff assemble authorization packets by hand, and most of that assembly is spent on documents that do not move the decision. Reading four of eleven, with a stated error bound, is the claim under evaluation.
